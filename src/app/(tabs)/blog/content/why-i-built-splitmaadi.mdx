After Splitwise introduced a daily cap on how many expenses you can add, I started searching for another alternative.

At first, this sounded like a simple problem. There are already plenty of expense-splitting apps, and group expense
tracking is not exactly a new category. But the more I looked, the more I realized that the product I wanted was
surprisingly hard to find.

My requirements were actually very basic:

1. **[P0]** It should not restrict how many expenses I can add.
2. **[P0]** It should not show ads.
3. **[P0]** It should not restrict what kind of expenses are allowed, especially if I want to attach receipts or other
   media.
4. **[P0]** It should not delete my past expense history.
5. **[P1]** It should have a clear and simple user experience.
6. **[P2]** It should work across all the devices I use — Mac, iPhone, Android, Windows, and everything else in between.

The closest alternatives were the built-in expense-sharing features inside payment apps like PhonePe and Paytm. They
were usable, but only up to a point. Adding even a simple expense took too many clicks, mainly because this feature was
hidden deep inside the app. These apps are not focused on expense splitting, since it is not their main feature. Desktop
support was almost non-existent. And attachment history was also not something I could rely on, since these apps would
delete it after a few months.

That was the point where I stopped searching and decided to build one myself: [SplitMaadi](https://splitmaadi.com).
_Maadi_ is a Kannada word that means “do it”.

## What I wanted to optimize for

I was not trying to build a feature-heavy finance app. I just wanted to build an expense-sharing app that is simple,
practical, and works well.

That led to a few clear product decisions.

### 1. No limits on the core use case

If the main purpose of the app is to let users add shared expenses, then limiting expense creation makes little sense.

SplitMaadi does not put limits on how many expenses you can add, how many groups you can create, or how many people you
can add to a group.

### 2. Simple user experience

One thing I care about a lot is reducing unnecessary friction.

If I am adding an expense, I do not want too many steps, too much clutter, or confusing flows. I want the app to feel
simple and easy to use.

### 3. Attachments should be supported properly

Expenses are not always just numbers. Sometimes you want to attach a receipt or an image for reference.

So I did not want attachments to feel like a secondary feature. They are part of the expense itself.

### 4. Cross-platform support

I also wanted it to work across all the devices I use.

Sometimes I may add an expense on my phone, and sometimes I may want to check balances later on my laptop. So being
cross-platform was an important requirement for me, not an extra nice-to-have.

## How SplitMaadi works across platforms

At its core, SplitMaadi is built as a **Progressive Web App (PWA)**. If a device can run a browser, it can run
SplitMaadi. That covers Mac, Windows, Linux, tablets, and pretty much anything else with a screen. On mobile, you can
install it to your home screen so it behaves like a regular app.

But I also wanted SplitMaadi to feel native on phones. So I built dedicated apps for both **iPhone** and **Android**
using Capacitor — which is essentially a thin native wrapper around the same web app. The underlying codebase is shared,
so the experience stays consistent across platforms.

Both apps are currently in review on the **App Store** and **Play Store**, and should be live within a few weeks. In the
meantime, the PWA works just fine on both platforms.

This setup gives me the best of both worlds: a single codebase that runs everywhere, and native apps for people who
prefer installing from their app store.

## What SplitMaadi tries to do differently

At a product level, SplitMaadi is not trying to win by adding complexity. It is trying to win by removing the common
sources of friction.

The goals are straightforward:

- let users add expenses without arbitrary caps
- keep the experience clean and fast
- support receipts and attachments properly
- work across devices
- preserve history instead of treating it as disposable

That is it.

In many ways, SplitMaadi is just an attempt to make expense sharing feel boring again — in the good sense. You should
not have to think about the tool too much. It should quietly do its job and stay reliable over time.

## Free to use

The most important part is this: **SplitMaadi is free to use, and I intend to keep it that way.**

No premium gate just to keep using the basic product. No daily cap on adding expenses. No ads getting in the way of the
experience.
